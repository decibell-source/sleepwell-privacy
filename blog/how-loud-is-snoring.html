How Loud Is Snoring? What's Normal?

Published March 21, 2026 · 4 min read

Not all snoring sounds the same. Some people produce a faint, rhythmic hum. Others generate sounds that can be heard through walls. But how loud is snoring, really — and when does loudness become a concern?

Snoring Loudness in Decibels

Snoring typically ranges from 40 dB to over 90 dB. To put that in perspective:

  • 40 dB — a quiet library. Light snoring falls in this range and is usually not disruptive.
  • 50-60 dB — normal conversation level. Moderate snoring that can disturb a sleeping partner.
  • 60-70 dB — a running vacuum cleaner. Heavy snoring that disrupts sleep in the same room.
  • 70-80 dB — a busy street. Very loud snoring that can be heard from adjacent rooms.
  • 80-90+ dB — a lawnmower or motorcycle. Extreme snoring, often associated with sleep apnea.

The loudest recorded snoring has reached over 90 dB — louder than a food blender. At this level, snoring isn't just a nuisance; it's a potential health signal.

What Affects Snoring Volume?

Airway narrowing

The more constricted the airway, the faster air must travel through it, and the more the surrounding tissues vibrate. A severely narrowed airway produces louder, more turbulent sound.

Sleeping position

Back sleeping tends to produce louder snoring because gravity pushes the tongue backward. Side sleeping often reduces both the frequency and volume of snoring.

Alcohol and sedatives

These substances relax throat muscles beyond their normal resting state, allowing more tissue collapse and louder vibration.

Congestion and inflammation

Swollen nasal passages or a blocked nose force mouth breathing, which typically results in louder snoring than nasal breathing.

Why Loudness Matters

Snoring volume isn't just about noise — it's a proxy for airway obstruction severity. Research has shown that:

  • Louder snoring correlates with a higher risk of obstructive sleep apnea
  • Snoring above 60 dB is more likely to fragment your partner's sleep
  • Increasing snoring volume over time may indicate worsening airway health

If your snoring regularly exceeds 60 dB or has been getting louder over time, it's worth tracking consistently and discussing with a healthcare professional.

How to Measure Your Snoring Loudness

You can't estimate your own snoring volume — you're asleep. But technology can help:

  • Sleep tracking apps — apps like SleepWell analyze audio levels throughout the night and categorize your snoring intensity
  • Decibel meter apps — a basic option, but they don't distinguish snoring from other sounds
  • Clinical sleep studies — the gold standard for measuring snoring as part of a broader sleep assessment

The advantage of a dedicated sleep app is that it can track your snoring volume over time, helping you spot trends that a single measurement would miss.

Conclusion

Snoring ranges from barely audible to startlingly loud. While light, occasional snoring is usually harmless, consistently loud snoring deserves attention — both for your health and for anyone sharing your bedroom. Measuring and tracking your snoring intensity is a simple step that can reveal a lot about your sleep quality.
